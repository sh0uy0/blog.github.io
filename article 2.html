What you need to know.

In our previous conversation, we learnt was on the basics of nutrition. We demystified some of the notions we have held to ourselves regarding what nutrition is and who a nutritionist is. We also highlighted on types of nutrients and got to list them as macro and micronutrients. Today we are going to focus on macronutrients, specifically carbohydrates. Hop on and let’s ride to a better understanding of what they are and why they are important for us. Remember we said macronutrients are required in large amounts and the body relies on them for energy provision. The energy required to fuel this train called our bodies is obtained from carbohydrates majorly. Carbohydrates consist of carbon, hydrogen and alcohol at the chemical level. Whenever I think of carbohydrates, the first thing that comes to mind is bread. I hope am not the only one. They are subdivided into different categories based on glucose units and chemical bonding of these glucose units. This includes the sugars, starches and fibre as we better know them.Most of the carbohydrates we consume are consumed as disaccharides such as milk and table sugar and polysaccharides in starches and fiber. Starches are made of many glucose units linked together as is in legumes and grains. Some starches resist digestion by enzymes but most are digested by enzymes in the body. The bonds linking to create fibre are resistant to digestion by enzymes. Sugars and starches yield glucose which is the main source of energy for the body. Glucose, when in excess is stored as glycogen in the muscle and liver. The liver can only store a little of this glucose and therefore the excess is converted as body fat which is stored in the body. I hope you now understand why it is important for us to control our carbohydrate portion in the diet. We don’t want excess fats depositing in the body. Women especially know how hard it is to dress that tummy or even those chubby thighs but if we can do something about it, even the better.

Carbohydrates apart from providing energy for use in form of glucose also provide stored energy as we have earlier said. Glycogen which is stored as excess glucose helps to preserve muscle.
When the body is lacking in glucose, glycogen is broken down into amino acids that are then converted to glucose for the provision of energy.

The process of digestion, therefore involves enzymes so they can be broken down to monosaccharides that the body can simply utilize. Starches are digested in the mouth with the help of salivary amylase (Biology , remember?). Hydrolysis of starch is fast because as we know food just goes to the mouth for chewing which takes a shorter period of time. It then proceeds to the stomach where at this time the salivary amylase enzymes are now denatured due to the acidic environment. Since dietary fibre is resistant to digestion by enzymes in the stomach and mouth, it then passes into the large intestines while intact. In the small intestine is where digestion bulkily takes place. The pancreatic amylase enzyme found here buffers the acidic food from the stomach and make the food accessible for digestion in the small intestine. The starch digestion is then completed and the food is broken down to simple sugars and the cells lining the small intestine absorb and carry them to the bloodstream. This makes it accessible for provision f energy, which as we now all know is the main function of energy for our body. If this is not reason enough why carbohydrates are important then I don’t know what is. Carbohydrates apart from providing energy for use in form of glucose also provide stored energy as we have earlier said. Glycogen which is stored as excess glucose helps to preserve muscle. When the body is lacking in glucose, specifically in times of starvation, these glycogen is broken down into amino acids that are then converted to glucose for the provision of energy. Starvation is not desirable but thanks to glycogen in storage we are able to get energy for the body function. It is therefore very crucial to include carbohydrates in the diet since the loss of muscle mass can affect other body functions. Other important functions of carbohydrates are mainly from dietary fibre which we shall discuss in our next post. Let’s make healthy food choices for a better tomorrow. Thank you for stopping by.
