Hello beautiful people. In our last post, we were looking at proteins as a macronutrient we need to incorporate in our diet. We also had a closer look at the importance of these proteins in our body. It goes without saying that we need proteins and especially those of a high biological value in our diet. In this post we have an insight into fats as the last macronutrient in our diet. So, what are fats? What is their importance in our diet? Sit still and be ready to read and be amazed by the wonder that fats are.
Fats are an important part of our diet. As much as they are important, we still need to understand that are the good fats and make the choice to include them in the diet. Now, which are these good fats and which are the bad ones? Fats are those that are solid in nature while those that are liquid in nature are called oils. The physical properties of fat in the diet determine how well our body utilizes them. The WHO recommends that less than 30% of our total energy intake should come from fats. This goes to enforce my earlier statement on choosing the right fats to incorporate them in the diet. We have fat soluble vitamins that we consume and they require fats for them to be absorbed in the body.
1 gram of fat yields 9 kilocalories of energy. This is why it is an important source of energy for body functions when taken in moderation. Unsaturated and saturated fats are terms that are interchangeably used in the public domain. The saturated fats are those that have long carbon chain structure whereas unsaturated contain one or more double bonds in their structure. Those that have one double bond are monounsaturated fats while those with multiple bonds are polyunsaturated fats. Good examples of polyunsaturated fatty acids are the omega oils which we have known to be good for important body functions and growth. There is no fat that is exclusively saturated or unsaturated. Each fat has a bit of each in different proportions.
At body temperature, both saturated and unsaturated fats are liquid temperature. At room temperature, saturated fats are solid in nature while unsaturated fats are liquid in nature. The double bond in unsaturated fats reacts with hydrogen in a process called hydrogenation. Extensive research shows that while saturated fats raise the bad cholesterol in the blood, this is not associated to higher mortality rate from cardiovascular diseases. Fats are therefore important part of a healthy diet when consumed in moderation because of the high caloric content they contain.

Important sources of saturated fats are mainly from animals and include fatty cuts of red meat, whole milk and its products like butter, cheese and cream, poultry skin and tropical oils such as coconut oil, palm oil and sunflower oil. Sources of unsaturated fats are avocado, olive oil, macadamia, peanuts, canola oil, local insects and from fish.
Fatty acids help the body by providing energy necessary for important body processes. They also supply fatty acids that are used in the cell membrane, retina, brain and in the endocrine system. Fats, specifically cholesterol, are a precursor in the manufacture of certain hormones. Fats are also important in the absorption of fat-soluble acids including the carotenes. It will be unfair to ignore the work of fat in providing insulation of the body from cold. Fats are good but only when taken in moderation.
