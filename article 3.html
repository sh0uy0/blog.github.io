Welcome back, good people. To bring you up to speed, we had a learning session on carbohydrates. We learnt about the different types there are and how important they are to include them in our diet. We also had some insights on how the digestion of carbohydrates takes place in the body. We were to continue talking about dietary fibre which is what today post is about. Let’s indulge.
So, dietary fibre! What is dietary fibre? It is in the group of complex carbohydrates but what differentiates it from the other groups is that it is not digested in the mouth and small intestine. Dietary fibre includes edible plants like fruits, vegetables, nuts and grains. It is further broken down to soluble and insoluble dietary fibre. Soluble dietary fibre, is soluble in water forming a gel. They mix with other food components and increase the viscosity of food passing the digestive tract.
What happens when we ingest dietary fibre is that the soluble part mixes with other food components decreasing the amount of sugars and cholesterol to be absorbed by the blood. When it gets to the large intestine, there is fermentation by bacteria and absorption and retention of important minerals such as calcium, zinc, magnesium and iron. The product of fermentation by these bacteria is what these bacteria rely on for nutrition. The physiological nature of dietary fibre is what forms the benefits of its consumption.
The insoluble dietary fibre in foods such as whole wheat and grains, bran and vegetables decrease passage time in the colon. They retain water and this increases the faecal mass, therefore reducing the incidence of constipation. Now you know why when constipated we are advised to take lots of vegetables and whole grains. In the upper digestive tract, soluble dietary fibre mixes with other nutrients delaying the gastric emptying of food in the small intestine and this makes one get the sensation of satiety, which is the feeling of fullness. When one feels full, it is because there was the release of the appetite-stimulating hormone and this helps in weight management. In order to reduce that extra weight we have gained over time, let's embrace dietary fibre in our diet.

Dietary fibre entraps nutrients such as cholesterol reducing the caloric density of food. This leads to lower blood cholesterol levels resulting in improved heart health. Research also shows that the binding of bile acids and salts by the dietary fibre reduces the risk of colon cancer.
It also shows that dietary fibre binds oestrogen, therefore inhibiting its reabsorption in the intestinal walls. There is therefore increased faecal oestrogen levels and subsequent lower levels of blood and urine oestrogen which reduces the risk of breast cancer.

Dietary fibre, according to researchers in different parts of the world reduces the risk of developing type 2 Diabetes and metabolic syndrome in addition to cardiovascular diseases. This is because the soluble dietary fibre increases the bulk of food due to entrapment of nutrients, therefore leading to a decrease in blood glucose and lipids. This lowers glycemic index and enhanced the insulin sensitivity leading to reduced risk of developing type 2 Diabetes. There is also reduced risk of developing diseases due to obesity some of which we have mentioned above.
