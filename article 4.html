Hello beautiful people. I hope the past week has been good to you and this new one is nothing but the best. We talked about dietary fibre in the last post and their importance in our diet. I hope we are challenged enough to add more of those vegetables and whole grains on our plate. According to a report by WHO, the prevalence of obesity has been tripling between 1975 and 2016 where 13% of the adults were obese and 39% were overweight. If these figures are anything to go by, dietary fibre needs to be an integral part of our diet from 2019 moving forward. This is particularly to help reduce the risk of developing cardiovascular diseases and other non-communicable diseases such as type 2 Diabetes among other health benefits in the earlier post.
Today we talk about proteins. Well, did my heart just skip? Probably because I think proteins are just the bomb. What are proteins, you ask. Proteins are macronutrients made of building blocks called amino acids. These amino acids have carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen and sometimes sulphur. Humans have non-essential amino acids in their system but require essential amino acids which are supplied by a diet that is rich in protein. You get why it is important to include proteins in our diet?
Proteins are classified as either complete or incomplete. Complete proteins are those that have all essential amino acids in their structure while incomplete proteins are lacking one or more of the essential amino acids. Incomplete proteins are found in plant proteins such as beans, peas, nuts and seeds. Complete proteins include proteins from animal sources such as meat, fish, chicken, eggs, milk, cheese, rice (6% protein), potatoes and soybean. When consumed alone, the body cannot fully utilize them for protein synthesis. To promote synthesis of proteins by the body, we are advised to take incomplete together with complete. A good combination that we normally consume is rice and beans.
The quality of these proteins is another important factor to consider when consuming proteins in the diet. Biological value is the measure for efficiency in which essential amino acids are incorporated in the tissues. To help in growth and repair, we need to take proteins of high biological value. The biological value of above 70 is considered good quality in cases especially where they are the only sources consumed. Low biological value proteins should not be consumed as the only sources because they consume low quantities of essential amino acids. Generally high biological value proteins include meats, fish, rice, eggs and milk. Recommended dietary intake depends on the age, BMI, pregnancy and physical activity among other factors.

Proteins make up the fundamental structure of body cells. They make up most of the body muscles, hair, skin, nails and an important part of regulatory substances such as enzymes, hormones and blood plasma. It also works to facilitate growth and repair of worn out or damaged tissues of the body as well as build new ones.

Protein digestion begins in the stomach and depends on the action of enzymes found in the stomach. These enzymes break down the protein molecules into amino acids. These amino acids move to the small intestines where enzymes found here break them down further and then they enter the duodenum and further breakdown continues to two or more amino acids. The small chains are absorbed through the intestinal lining into the bloodstream.

The membranes that are separating different fluids in and out of cells are not permeable which helps to maintain circulation within, without or between cells. The nature of proteins acts as a buffer when the blood pH reaches its extreme due to some bodily functions.
Proteins are the building blocks of white blood cells and antibodies that are parts of the infection-fighting properties of the body’s immune system. In times of starvation, when the body has insufficient carbohydrates and fats to provide energy, it utilizes energy stored as proteins by breaking it down to simpler carbohydrates that are used to provide energy for important body process. Enzymes that are responsible for the digestion of fats, proteins and carbohydrates are all protein in nature. Transport of oxygen throughout the circulatory system is made possible by haemoglobin which is protein in nature. Well, that’s all for today. Have a lovely week.
